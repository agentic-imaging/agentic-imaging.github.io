How it works

Turning raw measurements into a trustworthy image takes far more than calling a solver: forward models must be checked, hyperparameters tuned on the right split, metrics computed consistently, and every claim traced back to raw arrays. AgX runs that whole loop.

Deployment

Given a new imaging problem — forward model, data, goal — AgX adapts known solvers, tunes them honestly on validation data, and delivers verified reconstructions on held-out test data.

Discovery

AgX proposes candidate reconstruction-algorithm variants, benchmarks them under matched budgets, and reports which survive skeptical review — including the ones that don't.3

Full architecture and the two modes in depth → the technical report.

Honest by construction

The point of AgX is not a leaderboard — it is evidence you can trust. The verification and provenance machinery runs inside the loop, so an unverifiable or physics-inconsistent result is blocked, not reported.

Measurement fidelity

Image-space metrics alone can never approve a result. The relative residual ‖A(x̂)−y‖ / ‖y‖ must be consistent with the declared noise level, or the reconstruction is flagged as physics-inconsistent — however good its PSNR looks.4

Adversarial verification

A skeptical advisor watches every run and blocks reporting on missing artifacts, hash mismatches, or too-good-to-be-true results (triggering a dedicated attack budget). Code changes pass a multi-agent review before they execute.

Provenance & replay

Every reported number carries an immutable record — raw arrays, per-sample metrics, code/config hashes, compute environment, exact command. Figures cite records by id; replay tooling re-verifies hashes and recomputes metrics from frozen arrays.

Frozen ≠ publishable

A core rule: an artifact can be sealed, hashed, and reproducible and still not be a public claim until its evidence gate passes and a human approves it.

The verifier stack, provenance schema, and record format in full → the technical report.

Evidence

Public evidence comes from a preregistered, sealed, label-blind protocol: protocols, budgets, seeds, and stop rules are cryptographically sealed before held-out labels are revealed, and scoring happens once, after an authorized reveal. Until that completes and a human release gate signs off, this site shows no real evidence numbers.

Related work

AgX sits where three fast-moving lines of work meet — and takes a deliberate position within them.

ML for science. Machine learning is now a primary engine of discovery — protein structure and design (AlphaFold 3[1], crowned by the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry), materials (GNoME[2], MatterGen[3]), weather (GraphCast[4], GenCast[5] — now operational at ECMWF), and mathematics (FunSearch[6], AlphaProof[7]) — increasingly organized around scientific foundation models.

Agentic AI for research. The frontier is autonomous: “AI-scientist” systems (Sakana AI[8], Google’s AI co-scientist[9]) and agent-run chemistry and materials labs (Coscientist[10], ChemCrow[11], A-Lab[12]). Yet research-agent benchmarks stay low — MLE-bench[13] ~17%, ScienceAgentBench[14] ~32%, CORE-Bench[15] ~19% on the hardest tasks — and documented failures (reward-hacked evaluations, contested “discoveries,” irreproducible results) are common.

The science of ML — and a rigor crisis. As ML becomes an object of study (scaling laws, interpretability, the unsettled “emergence” debate[16]), it inherits the failures it imports elsewhere: leakage-driven irreproducibility[17] across hundreds of papers, and a wave of AI-generated “slop” straining peer review.

The common bottleneck is verification: generation has outpaced validation. AgX’s response is to fold skepticism into the loop — measurement-fidelity checks, enforced validation/test discipline, adversarial multi-agent review, and provenance-locked results — so that connecting scientists to the best of AI/ML also yields results they can trust.

Citations are current as of mid-2026; some results are actively disputed — note the A-Lab author correction[12] walking back novelty claims, and the unresolved emergence debate[16].

Ethics & social impact

Why it matters. Autonomous scientific agents can produce figures, metrics, and even papers far faster than anyone can check them — and the field is already paying for it in reward-hacked benchmarks, irreproducible findings, and AI-generated “slop.” An agent that optimizes a number while ignoring the physics, the data split, or the provenance is not a scientific tool; it is a liability.

Our stance. AgX is built so that speed does not cost trust: every reported number binds to raw artifacts and a provenance record; measurement-fidelity and validation/test discipline are enforced, not optional; results are cross-examined by adversarial verifiers; and publication stays a human-gated act — the automation prepares the evidence, a person approves it.

Limits and dual use. AgX accelerates the search over methods; it does not replace domain expertise, experimental validation, or peer review, and its outputs are proposals to be checked, not settled facts. The same automation that speeds honest work could amplify careless or bad-faith work — which is precisely why we invest in verification, not just generation.

Access. We want the interface itself to be usable by as many people as possible; see the accessibility statement.
